For retirees with meaningful fixed-income allocations, the choice between holding **individual bonds** (typically structured as a "ladder") vs. holding a **bond fund or ETF** is a real planning question. Both approaches deliver fixed-income exposure. They differ in ways that matter for retirees.

Here's the honest comparison.

## Bond funds and ETFs

A bond fund holds hundreds or thousands of individual bonds in a single pooled vehicle. You buy shares of the fund. The fund manages the underlying bonds — buying new issues as old ones mature, selling as conditions warrant. Examples: Vanguard Total Bond Market (BND), iShares Core US Aggregate (AGG), short/intermediate/long duration variants.

**Advantages:**

- **Diversification.** A single ETF gives exposure to thousands of issuers across maturities, sectors, and credit qualities.
- **Liquidity.** Sell a share at any market open. Easy.
- **Convenience.** No bond-by-bond administration. The manager handles maturity replacement, defaults (if any), and reinvestment.
- **Low minimums.** Useful for smaller accounts.
- **Predictable cost.** Expense ratios for major bond ETFs run 0.03%–0.10%.

**Disadvantages:**

- **Net asset value fluctuates.** Bond fund prices move daily based on interest rates. In a rising-rate environment, the fund's price drops; you can sell at a loss even if you "wanted to hold to maturity" — but the fund doesn't have a maturity date.
- **No specific maturity.** You can't say "I'll have $50,000 on October 1, 2030 to fund my expense for that year." The fund's holdings have varying maturities and constantly turn over.
- **Limited income predictability.** The yield drifts as the underlying portfolio changes.

## Individual bond ladders

A bond ladder is a portfolio of individual bonds with **staggered maturities** — typically equal weights maturing each year over 5–10 years. As each year's bonds mature, the principal can be either spent (perfect for retirees) or rolled into a new bond at the long end of the ladder.

**Advantages:**

- **Predictable cash flows.** You know exactly when each principal payment will arrive.
- **Hold to maturity.** You're not at the mercy of intervening price movements. As long as the issuer doesn't default, you receive face value at maturity regardless of what interest rates have done.
- **Income matching.** A ladder can be designed to produce specific cash flows in specific years — useful for retirees with specific spending plans.
- **No expense ratio.** Direct holdings have zero ongoing fund cost.
- **Tax control.** Individual bonds can be sold strategically; municipal bonds let you control the location of tax-exempt income; tax-loss harvesting is possible at the bond level.

**Disadvantages:**

- **Higher per-issue cost.** Buying small lots of individual bonds historically came with wider bid-ask spreads. This has improved but still exists, particularly for corporate and municipal bonds.
- **Concentration risk.** A 10-bond ladder has 10 issuers. A bond fund has thousands. If one of your individual issuers defaults, you take a real hit.
- **Administration.** Tracking maturities, reinvesting proceeds, monitoring credit quality. More work than a fund.
- **Minimums matter.** Bond minimums are typically $1,000–$5,000 per issue. A 10-year ladder with reasonable diversification (3–5 issues per rung) requires $300K+ to do well.
- **Active monitoring.** Credit ratings change. Issuers can call bonds early. Some attention is required.

## The retiree case

For retirees specifically, a few features push the ladder approach into stronger consideration:

**1. Cash flow matching.** A retiree spending $80K/year from the bond portfolio benefits from knowing each year's $80K will arrive when needed — regardless of what the broader bond market is doing. A bond ladder makes this explicit.

**2. Sequence-of-returns insulation.** [Sequence-of-returns risk](/articles/sequence-of-returns-risk) is most damaging when retirees are forced to sell during a downturn. Holding individual bonds to maturity sidesteps the "selling at a loss" problem entirely for the ladder portion.

**3. The "bucket strategy" mindset.** Many retirees mentally separate their portfolio into near-term spending money (years 1–5), intermediate-term (years 6–15), and long-term (years 15+). A bond ladder maps cleanly to the near-term bucket.

**4. Treasury and municipal options.** For HNW retirees in higher tax brackets, municipal bonds offer tax-exempt income that's hard to replicate via funds at scale. Treasury ladders eliminate credit risk entirely.

## The bond fund case

Several scenarios where a fund is the better choice:

**Smaller accounts.** Below ~$300K of fixed income allocation, the diversification cost of individual bonds is real. Use a fund.

**Younger investors.** A 35-year-old with bond exposure isn't trying to match cash flows — they want diversified bond returns. Fund.

**Investors who don't want the administration.** A ladder is more work. If you don't want to manage it (or pay an advisor to), a fund is simpler.

**Tax-deferred accounts.** The tax efficiency arguments for individual munis don't apply inside an IRA or 401(k). Funds are usually fine there.

**International or specialty bond exposure.** Hard to build a diversified emerging-market debt or high-yield exposure with individual bonds. Funds excel here.

## What we generally do

For client retirees with substantial fixed-income allocations (typically $500K+):

- **Treasury or high-quality muni ladder** for the near-term spending bucket (5–10 years of expenses)
- **Bond fund or ETF** for the longer-duration intermediate and long buckets
- **Hold-to-maturity discipline** on the ladder portion — we don't sell rungs prematurely
- **Annual review** of the ladder structure as rates and circumstances shift

This combines the cash-flow predictability of the ladder with the diversification and convenience of funds for the longer-horizon bond exposure. It's more work than just "buy BND," but for a retiree with meaningful fixed income and specific cash flow needs, the work earns its keep.

## The interest-rate environment matters

The case for individual bonds is much stronger when yields are reasonable. Building a ladder with 4–5% Treasury yields is meaningfully different from building one with 1% Treasury yields. If yields fall significantly from current levels, the ladder vs. fund tradeoff narrows.

In the current environment (mid-decade 2020s with elevated yields), individual bonds have rarely been more competitive vs. funds for retirees. This may not always be the case.

## What to ask

If your advisor recommends a bond fund for your retirement portfolio without considering whether a ladder might be appropriate, that's worth a conversation. The default isn't always wrong — but it should be a deliberate choice, not a habit.
