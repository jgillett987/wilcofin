Periodically a client asks why we don't move portfolios to cash before a downturn, or rotate aggressively into whatever's working. The honest answer is that we've looked carefully at what market timing actually delivers in practice, and the evidence against it is overwhelming. This isn't a philosophical position. It's a conclusion from 90+ years of data.

## What "market timing" actually is

Market timing means making investment decisions based on a forecast of where markets will go in the near term. Move to cash because a recession is coming. Pile into tech because the Fed is about to cut. Buy gold because inflation is back. Dump small-caps because they've underperformed.

It's the act of changing your portfolio based on a prediction about the next 1–18 months.

## What the data shows

Three lines of evidence converge:

**1. Professional managers don't beat the market consistently.** SPIVA reports (the gold standard for active manager performance tracking) consistently show that 80–90% of actively managed U.S. equity funds underperform their benchmark over 15-year periods. The persistence of outperformance is essentially zero — past winners don't predict future winners. If professionals with full-time research teams can't time markets, the assumption that retail investors or part-time advisors can is implausible.

**2. The biggest market days drive most of the long-term return.** Studies consistently show that missing the 10 best market days over a 20-year period reduces the total return by roughly 50%. The 10 best days tend to occur during volatile periods — often within weeks of the worst days. Timing requires being right *twice*: when to get out, and when to get back in. Investors who get out tend to stay out too long.

**3. Investor returns lag fund returns.** Morningstar's "Mind the Gap" study documents that average dollar-weighted investor returns are roughly 1.5% per year lower than the funds they invest in — entirely because of poor timing decisions (buying after gains, selling after losses). The very act of trying to time creates a return drag.

## Why timing is so hard

The structural problem with market timing is that you have to be right about three things, in sequence:

1. **What's coming** — economic, political, technical conditions
2. **How markets will respond** — markets often move against the obvious narrative
3. **When others will respond** — being early is functionally identical to being wrong

Even legendary investors — Buffett, Munger, others — explicitly disclaim market timing. They make valuation-based decisions over multi-year horizons, which is a different activity. "I think the market is overvalued" is not the same as "I'm moving to cash next week."

## Why people keep trying anyway

Several persistent reasons:

**Confirmation bias.** People remember the time they "called" 2008 or "knew" 2020 was coming. They forget the dozens of false alarms in between. Hindsight makes timing look easier than it is in real time.

**Survivorship bias in commentary.** Financial media features the manager who got the last call right. The 47 managers who got it wrong don't get airtime.

**Discomfort with volatility.** Sitting still during a 25% drawdown is psychologically harder than acting. Acting feels productive, even when it isn't.

**Sales incentives.** Brokers, financial media, and investment newsletters all benefit from generating action. A boring "stay invested" message doesn't sell subscriptions or generate trading commissions.

## What we do instead

Two things, both unrelated to timing:

**1. Construct portfolios appropriate to client circumstances.** Asset allocation should reflect goals, time horizon, and tolerance for drawdowns. A 65-year-old retiree with $2M and modest spending has a different right portfolio than a 35-year-old with 30 years of accumulation ahead. We model these explicitly.

**2. Manage costs and tax efficiency.** Fees compound. Tax drag compounds. Rebalancing decisions interact with both. Across a multi-decade horizon, getting the boring fundamentals right adds 1%+ of net annual return — every year, reliably, without needing to predict anything.

This is unsexy. It doesn't generate quarterly performance discussions about how we positioned brilliantly for the latest move. But it's what actually compounds.

## The exceptions worth noting

A few situations that look like market timing but aren't:

**Rebalancing.** Selling appreciated assets and buying lagging ones to maintain target allocations is mechanical and rules-based. Not timing.

**Tax-loss harvesting.** Realizing losses to offset gains is opportunistic but not directional — we're not predicting the market, we're harvesting the realized variance that already happened. See [Tax-Loss Harvesting](/articles/tax-loss-harvesting).

**Major life-event reallocation.** Significantly changing the portfolio because circumstances changed (retirement, inheritance, major asset sale) is planning, not timing.

**Valuation-driven multi-year tilts.** Modestly adjusting allocations based on long-term valuation signals (e.g., trimming equity exposure when broad market valuations reach historical extremes) has some empirical support — but the effect is small, the signal is noisy, and it's a tilt at the margin, not a wholesale move.

## What we tell clients during drawdowns

The honest answer in a market drop is usually some version of: "We expected this. Drawdowns are part of the deal. Your plan is designed to withstand them. The wrong move is to react to it."

Followed by the practical question: "Is anything in your life genuinely different now? If not, the right action is no action."

This is harder to say than it sounds when a portfolio is down 25%. But over 30+ years of investing, it's the action — or non-action — that actually compounds wealth.

## Where this leaves us

Investing well is mostly about doing a few obvious things consistently for a long time, and avoiding the temptations that look like activity but produce only friction. Market timing is the most expensive form of that friction.

We don't time markets. We construct sensible portfolios, manage them efficiently, and stay invested. Across a multi-decade horizon, that's what works.
