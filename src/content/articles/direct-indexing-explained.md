Over the last five years, "direct indexing" has gone from an institutional tool to a heavily marketed retail product. The basic idea is sound, the tax benefits are real for some investors, and the value gets oversold to others. Here's the honest framework.

## The mechanic

Instead of buying a single index ETF (say, VOO for the S&P 500), direct indexing buys the **underlying individual stocks** that compose the index — typically several hundred names — in proportions that approximate the index's weights.

The portfolio's market exposure is essentially the same as the ETF's. The fees are higher (more on that below), but the structure unlocks one specific benefit: the ability to harvest tax losses at the **individual stock level** rather than only at the fund level.

## The tax benefit, illustrated

Imagine the S&P 500 returns +10% over a year. Inside the index, some stocks are up 40%, some are down 25%, most are somewhere in between.

If you hold VOO, you have one position up 10%. No losses to harvest.

If you hold a direct-indexed S&P 500 portfolio, you have hundreds of positions — some up substantially, some down. The losers can be sold to harvest the loss (offsetting other capital gains in your portfolio), and the proceeds reinvested in similar but not identical names to maintain market exposure.

In a year like this, a direct-indexed portfolio can generate **$5K–$30K of harvested losses per $1M invested** even when the overall index is up. That loss carries forward indefinitely and can offset future gains.

## What it's actually worth

Academic and industry research roughly converges on:

- **0.3%–1.0% per year of additional after-tax return** for typical taxable investors in the early years of a direct-indexed portfolio
- **Diminishing benefit over time** — once you've harvested most of the available losses, the well runs dry
- **Higher benefit in volatile years** (2022 was a great year for direct indexing) and lower benefit in low-volatility bull markets
- **Higher benefit in higher tax brackets** — the harvested losses are worth more when you're paying more on gains

For a high-bracket taxable investor in the first 5–10 years of a direct-indexed portfolio, the genuine benefit is meaningful — well worth the additional fee.

## The fees

Direct indexing platforms typically charge **0.10%–0.40% per year** above the underlying index ETF cost. Compare to a low-cost S&P 500 ETF at 0.03%, you're paying an extra 0.10–0.37% for the harvesting service.

For the strategy to be economic, the harvested loss value (in present-value terms, accounting for the basis reduction in the replacement security) needs to exceed that fee. For high-bracket investors with a long horizon, it usually does. For lower-bracket or shorter-horizon investors, it often doesn't.

## When it makes sense

- **Taxable accounts** (the entire benefit is tax-driven; doesn't apply in IRAs)
- **High marginal tax rates** (the harvested losses are worth more)
- **Long holding horizons** (more years of compounding the harvest benefit)
- **Existing realized gains to offset** — particularly useful when [unwinding a concentrated stock position](/articles/concentrated-stock-position) or selling a business
- **Households doing meaningful charitable giving** — eventually gifting appreciated direct-indexed positions multiplies the benefit
- **Account balances above ~$200K** where the per-stock allocation is large enough for harvesting to matter

## When it doesn't make sense

- **Inside an IRA, 401(k), Roth, or other tax-deferred account.** No taxable gains to offset. Just use a low-cost ETF.
- **Small accounts** (below ~$100K). The fixed cost of the platform doesn't earn enough harvest to justify the fee.
- **Short holding horizons.** If you'll sell the whole position within 3 years, the benefit doesn't compound enough.
- **Investors who don't have other capital gains to offset.** Harvested losses without gains are limited to $3,000/year against ordinary income.
- **Investors planning to hold positions until death and use the [step-up in basis](/articles/step-up-in-basis).** The harvest reduces basis; the step-up at death wipes out the gain regardless. Direct indexing is net-neutral or slightly negative in this case.

## What it doesn't do

A few things direct indexing is sometimes pitched as solving but doesn't:

- **It doesn't beat the index.** Pre-tax returns are essentially identical to the underlying ETF, minus fees. The benefit is tax-deferral, not alpha.
- **It doesn't customize your portfolio in any meaningful way** beyond optional ESG screens or single-stock exclusions. You're still tracking the index.
- **It doesn't eliminate tax** — it defers it. Every harvest reduces basis on the replacement security, increasing the eventual gain when sold.

## The marketing problem

Some direct indexing platforms quote "tax alpha" numbers in the 1.5%–2% range. Those numbers are usually first-year nominal harvest values, not net-of-basis-reduction long-term benefits. The honest economic benefit is closer to 0.3%–1.0% per year on average over a long horizon.

Still real, still worth doing in the right scenarios. Just smaller than the marketing suggests.

## Where Wilco lands

For high-bracket clients with substantial taxable accounts and existing capital gains needs (concentrated stock unwinds, business sales, ongoing rebalancing), we use direct indexing strategies on Schwab's platform when the math works. For clients without those characteristics — or for whom the same dollars could go into Roth or other tax-advantaged buckets first — a low-cost index ETF is usually the better choice.

The framing question we ask: "Is this dollar going into a taxable account that will accumulate gains over a long horizon, with realized gains we can offset?" If yes, direct indexing earns its fee. If no, simpler is better.
