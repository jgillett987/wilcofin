For high-earning Southwest Airlines pilots, one of the most powerful — and least understood — wealth-building features of the 401(k) is the **IRS Section 415(c) limit**. It caps total annual contributions to a single 401(k) plan at $70,000 in 2025 ($77,500 with the standard age 50+ catch-up, or higher with the new SECURE 2.0 super-catch-up at age 60–63).

For pilots with the cash flow to fund up to that limit and a plan that allows after-tax contributions with in-plan Roth conversions, the after-tax bucket adds tens of thousands per year of new Roth space. This is the math worth knowing.

## The three contribution buckets, recapped

Inside a single 401(k):

- **Pre-tax employee deferral**: $23,500 (2025), or $31,000 with age 50+ catch-up
- **Roth employee deferral**: same combined limit as pre-tax — you choose the split
- **After-tax contributions**: anything additional, up to the 415(c) ceiling

The total of all three, plus any employer contributions (match + profit sharing), cannot exceed the 415(c) limit. See [The SWA 401(k) Decision: Pre-Tax, Roth, or After-Tax?](/articles/swa-profit-sharing-decision) for the full framework on splitting.

## Working the math for a senior pilot

Take a senior SWA captain in 2025 making $400K with a strong profit-sharing year. Roughly:

- 415(c) limit: $77,500 (with age 50+ catch-up)
- Employee deferral (Roth-leaning): $31,000
- Employer match + profit sharing: ~$30,000 (varies by year)
- **Remaining 415(c) headroom for after-tax contributions: ~$16,500**

That's $16,500 of additional after-tax contributions that can be converted to Roth in-plan, generating $16,500 of new Roth space in a single year — on top of the regular contributions.

Over 10 years at age 55–65, with growth, that's an additional $250K–$400K of Roth balance at retirement.

## Why this matters more for pilots than for most professionals

Three reasons:

**1. SWA contributes meaningfully on your behalf.** Profit sharing varies but can be substantial. The employer contributions count against the 415(c) ceiling but cost you nothing — they reduce the after-tax bucket headroom but they're "free" dollars going in.

**2. Your earning trajectory peaks late.** Most pilots' biggest earning years are 50–65. That's exactly when the 415(c) optimization matters most.

**3. The age 50+ catch-up plus the new SECURE 2.0 super-catch-up at age 60–63 ($11,250 additional in 2025) make the late-career 415(c) limit higher than many realize.** A pilot at age 62 in 2025 has a much larger contribution ceiling than the same pilot at age 49.

## The plan rule that gates everything

The mega-backdoor strategy only works if your SWA 401(k) plan **specifically allows**:

1. **After-tax contributions** (separate from Roth deferrals) above the standard elective limit
2. **In-plan Roth conversions** OR **in-service withdrawals** to a Roth IRA

Most major-airline plans permit both, but verify with HR or by reading the Summary Plan Description. Two things to confirm specifically:

- Are after-tax contributions permitted? Some plans only allow Roth deferrals, which max at $23,500.
- Are in-plan Roth conversions automatic, on demand, or only annually?

If the plan permits both, you're set up. If not, the strategy doesn't apply.

## The execution sequence

For a high-earning pilot maxing 415(c) optimization:

**Early in the year (January–April):**

1. Set Roth elective deferral percentage high enough to hit the $23,500 / $31,000 cap by mid-year. Front-loading captures more market exposure during the year.
2. Begin after-tax contributions through HR's separate election (different from the Roth deferral election).
3. Set automatic in-plan Roth conversion if the plan allows it. This converts after-tax contributions to Roth as soon as they arrive — minimizing taxable earnings on the after-tax basis between contribution and conversion.

**Mid-year:**

1. Monitor remaining 415(c) headroom as profit-sharing contributions arrive (typically front-loaded in some plans, distributed throughout in others).
2. Adjust after-tax contribution rate if approaching the ceiling.

**End of year:**

1. Verify all conversions completed cleanly. Any after-tax balance still in the plan at year-end should be flagged for next-year follow-up.

## The earnings-on-after-tax wrinkle

After-tax contributions can sit in the 401(k) for some time before being converted to Roth. Any earnings that accrue on the after-tax basis during that window are pre-tax — and convert to Roth as a taxable event when the conversion happens.

For pilots whose plan permits **immediate** in-plan conversions (the same payroll cycle the contribution is made), this wrinkle is essentially zero. For plans where conversions only happen annually, several thousand dollars of taxable conversion income can accrue over the year.

The right execution: ask HR to enable automatic, immediate in-plan conversions on after-tax contributions. Most modern plan administrators support this. If yours doesn't, request quarterly or even monthly conversions to minimize the earnings drag.

## When this isn't the right move

A few pilots for whom maximizing 415(c) isn't the priority:

- **Pilots with cash flow constraints** — funding $77K in 401(k) requires meaningful surplus income above other obligations
- **Pilots paying down high-interest debt** — paying off a mortgage or other debt may earn a better risk-free return than the after-tax contribution
- **Pilots whose plan doesn't permit after-tax contributions or in-plan conversions** — strategy doesn't apply
- **Pilots already heavily Roth-weighted** with limited pre-tax balance — additional Roth marginal value diminishes; some pre-tax remains useful for QCD optimization later

For most senior pilots with the cash flow and the plan features, however, this is one of the highest-leverage moves available — quietly building hundreds of thousands of additional Roth wealth without disrupting cash flow.

## What to ask HR

If you're not sure whether your SWA plan supports the strategy:

1. "Does the plan allow after-tax (non-Roth, non-pre-tax) contributions?"
2. "What is the maximum after-tax contribution percentage I can elect?"
3. "Does the plan allow in-plan Roth conversions of after-tax contributions?"
4. "How frequently are in-plan conversions processed — automatic, monthly, quarterly, annually, on request?"

Once you have those answers, the optimization sequence is straightforward.
