For retirees who stop working before 65, the gap between leaving employer health coverage and Medicare eligibility is one of the most expensive line items of early retirement — unless they understand how the ACA marketplace works. Done thoughtfully, the same household can spend $24,000+ per year on COBRA or pay closer to $0 in premium subsidies. The difference is income management.

## The system, briefly

The Affordable Care Act marketplace (healthcare.gov in most states; some states run their own exchanges) offers individual health insurance plans. Premiums are subsidized via a federal tax credit (technically the Premium Tax Credit, or PTC) that's calculated based on **modified adjusted gross income (MAGI)**.

Lower MAGI = larger subsidy. Higher MAGI = smaller subsidy. Above certain thresholds, no subsidy.

The Inflation Reduction Act (extended through 2025 at this writing, with possible extension under negotiation) **eliminated the income cliff** that previously denied subsidies entirely above 400% of the Federal Poverty Level. Currently, the subsidy phases out gradually, capping the household contribution at 8.5% of MAGI for any income level — but the practical sweet spot for most early retirees is well below that.

## The numbers, illustrated

Consider a 62-year-old couple retiring early in Tennessee. They have $1.2M in taxable savings, $1.5M in pre-tax IRA, $300K in Roth, and no Social Security yet (delaying to 70).

**Scenario A: Live off pre-tax IRA distributions.**

Annual income (gross taxable): $90,000 (drawing from IRA for spending). MAGI roughly $90K. Premium tax credit: minimal. Annual ACA premium for a Silver plan: ~$22,000.

**Scenario B: Live off taxable savings (basis), small IRA withdrawal for low brackets.**

Annual income: $30,000 ordinary (small IRA distribution), $40,000 of long-term capital gains (basis-heavy taxable sales). MAGI roughly $70K. Premium tax credit: significantly higher. Annual ACA premium: ~$8,000–$12,000 depending on plan and ages.

Same household. Same lifestyle. ~$10,000–$14,000 annual difference in healthcare cost, just from how the income is structured.

Multiplied across 5+ years between early retirement and Medicare, that's $50K–$70K in real money.

## The income management strategy

Three principles for the bridge years:

**1. Draw heavily from taxable accounts with high basis.** Selling positions with low embedded gains generates spending money without inflating MAGI dramatically. Capital gains count toward MAGI but are calculated only on the gain portion, not the principal.

**2. Use Roth contributions (not earnings) tactically.** Roth IRA contribution withdrawals are tax-free, penalty-free, and don't appear in MAGI. Useful spending source that doesn't affect ACA subsidies.

**3. Defer pre-tax IRA distributions when possible.** Every dollar of traditional IRA withdrawal goes straight into MAGI at full ordinary-income value, reducing subsidies and triggering ACA cliffs.

This is the opposite of the conventional [tax-loss-harvesting era's](/articles/tax-loss-harvesting) "spend taxable first, then pre-tax, then Roth." For ACA-bridge years, the order shifts because the subsidy math overrides the conventional withdrawal sequence logic.

## The Roth conversion conflict

[Roth conversions](/articles/roth-conversion-decade) and ACA subsidies actively conflict. Each dollar of conversion adds to MAGI, which reduces subsidies. The math:

- A $50,000 Roth conversion at marginal rate 22% costs $11,000 in conversion tax
- Same conversion may cost $5,000–$15,000 in lost ACA subsidy
- True economic cost of conversion: $16,000–$26,000, not $11,000

That changes the conversion math substantially. For pre-Medicare retirees, the cost of conversions is meaningfully higher than for retirees on Medicare, where IRMAA is the main interaction (less expensive than ACA subsidy loss for most income levels).

The right move depends on:

- How many years until Medicare?
- How much pre-tax balance to convert?
- What's the marginal subsidy lost per conversion dollar?
- Will future ACA / Medicare rules change?

For many pre-Medicare early retirees, the right answer is **smaller conversions during the bridge years and larger conversions once on Medicare** — even though the bracket math alone might suggest the opposite.

## The "subsidy cliff" — historically a major issue

Before the Inflation Reduction Act, the ACA had a hard cliff at 400% of the Federal Poverty Level. Earn $1 over the threshold and you lost the entire subsidy — sometimes thousands of dollars in a single dollar of income. This created brutal effective marginal rates in the cliff zone.

The IRA temporarily eliminated the cliff, capping household premium contribution at 8.5% of MAGI regardless of income. This is set to expire at the end of 2025 unless extended. If the cliff returns, planning around it becomes much more important — particularly for households whose income comes close to the threshold.

Status here is moving — verify current rules at the time of planning.

## The HSA interaction

If you've been [funding an HSA aggressively during working years](/articles/hsa-stealth-retirement-account), the bridge years are a natural time to deploy it. HSA distributions for qualified medical expenses don't appear in MAGI — they're entirely tax-free. Using HSA funds for medical costs in the bridge years preserves taxable assets and keeps MAGI low.

Plus, ACA-marketplace HDHPs typically remain HSA-eligible, so contributions can continue if you maintain an HSA-qualified plan during the bridge years (until age 65 / Medicare).

## The state variation

ACA marketplace details vary by state:

- Some states (California, New York, others) run their own exchanges with additional subsidies
- Plan availability and pricing varies dramatically by region
- Some states have expanded Medicaid (which affects the lowest income tiers)

For Tennessee specifically, the federal exchange applies (healthcare.gov), Medicaid expansion has not occurred, and pricing depends heavily on county. Major metro areas (Nashville, Memphis, Knoxville) generally have more plan choices than rural counties.

## What to do

For households planning to retire before 65:

1. **Model the bridge years** explicitly. What's the income profile each year between retirement and Medicare?
2. **Identify the optimal MAGI target** — usually well below 400% FPL pre-IRA, now flexible under IRA rules
3. **Sequence withdrawals** to hit that target — taxable basis sales first, Roth contributions tactically, IRA distributions only as needed
4. **Coordinate with [Roth conversion strategy](/articles/roth-conversion-decade)** — the bridge years may not be the optimal conversion years
5. **Monitor ACA rule changes** annually — this is a politically active area

Most early retirees we work with end up structuring spending differently than their pre-Medicare default would have suggested, with meaningful annual savings on healthcare premiums.
