When you hire a financial advisor, two distinct functions get bundled in your mind: **advice** and **custody**. They're separate. Understanding the difference — and why the separation matters — is one of the most important pieces of investor protection that most people never think about.

## The distinction

**Advice** is the recommendation: what to invest in, how to allocate, when to rebalance, how to plan around tax events.

**Custody** is the physical (or digital) holding of your money: the institution that has your assets, processes the trades, sends the statements, and is technically the legal account holder.

A well-structured advisory relationship has these two functions performed by **different organizations**. Your advisor recommends and executes; an independent custodian actually holds the money.

## Why the separation matters

This isn't theoretical. The most spectacular financial fraud in modern history — Bernie Madoff — succeeded specifically because he combined advice and custody in the same entity. Clients sent money to "Madoff" and Madoff sent statements back. There was no independent third party verifying that the assets actually existed. They didn't.

A registered investment adviser using an independent custodian cannot construct a Madoff-style fraud. The advisor never has direct access to your money. The custodian's records are independent. You can verify your balance directly with the custodian at any time, regardless of what the advisor says.

This is one of the structural protections built into the modern RIA model — and it works as long as the advisor uses a real third-party custodian.

## The major custodians

The dominant custodians for U.S. RIAs:

**Charles Schwab** — Largest custodian in the world, over $10 trillion in client assets. Acquired TD Ameritrade in 2023, consolidating the RIA custody market. Robust technology, broad investment access, no transaction fees on stocks and ETFs, deep institutional resources. Most major RIAs custody at Schwab.

**Fidelity** — Strong custody platform, particularly for institutional and retirement accounts. Excellent technology. Long-term competitor to Schwab in this market.

**Pershing (BNY Mellon)** — Bank-trust focused, often used by independent broker-dealers and some RIAs.

**Interactive Brokers** — Lower-cost option, technology-forward, popular with sophisticated DIY investors and some RIAs.

A few smaller custodians serve specific niches.

- We have **trading authority** (we can place orders) and **fee-deduction authority** (we can withdraw our quarterly advisory fee)

If we disappeared tomorrow, you'd still have all your money at Schwab. You'd need to find another advisor (or manage it yourself), but the assets would be safe and untouched.

## The questions worth asking any advisor

When evaluating a financial advisor, three custody-related questions should always be asked:

**1. Who is your custodian?**

Look for a recognized name (Schwab, Fidelity, Pershing). If the answer is "we hold it here" or names the advisor's own firm, that's a major red flag. There are legitimate exceptions for very large institutional managers, but for individual HNW relationships, the custodian should be independent.

**2. Will the account be in my name, with me as the legal account holder?**

Yes is the right answer. Anything else (the advisor as account holder, a pooled investment vehicle, etc.) is structurally different and changes your protection.

**3. What withdrawal authority do you have?**

The acceptable answers are: trade authority on the account, fee-deduction authority for the advisory fee, and the ability to send money back to you (your linked bank). Any broader withdrawal authority — particularly the ability to send money to third parties — should be a serious concern.

If an advisor is uncomfortable answering these questions clearly and in writing, that's information about the advisor.

## What custodians actually do (and don't do)

A common misconception: the custodian "approves" the trades or "vets" the advisor. They don't.

Custodians:

- Hold the assets
- Execute trades the advisor places
- Process money movement
- Send statements
- Provide tax reporting (1099s)
- Maintain SIPC insurance on cash and securities (up to $500K per account, including $250K in cash)
- Monitor for unusual activity (regulatory requirement, but limited in scope)

Custodians do **not**:

- Verify the advisor is competent
- Verify the strategy is appropriate
- Endorse the advisor's recommendations
- Protect against bad investment decisions

The custodian's job is operational integrity, not investment quality. Bad investments can happen at the best custodian. The custodian's role is to make sure the bad investments are recorded accurately, settled correctly, and visible to you in real time.

## The "platform" question

Some advisor relationships add a layer between you and the custodian — a "platform" or "wrap account" structure. This typically adds:

- An additional administrative fee (often 0.10%–0.50%)
- Sometimes restrictions on which investments are available
- Sometimes additional reporting tools

Whether this is worth the cost depends on what the platform actually delivers. For most fee-only RIAs, direct custody at Schwab or Fidelity without an additional platform layer is the more efficient structure. For some advisors using third-party investment models, a platform may be unavoidable.

The question worth asking: is there a platform fee on top of the advisory fee, and what specifically does it provide that the custodian doesn't?
