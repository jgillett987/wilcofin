A Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) is one of the more sophisticated tools in HNW planning. Done well, it solves a specific set of problems elegantly: a large appreciated asset, a desire for lifetime income, a charitable goal, and a substantial tax bill that would otherwise come due on sale. Done in the wrong situation, it's expensive complexity that doesn't earn its keep.

Here's the honest framework.

## The structure

A CRT works in three phases:

**1. Funding.** You contribute a highly appreciated asset (usually stock or real estate) to the trust. The trust is irrevocable.

**2. Income phase.** The trust pays you (or another beneficiary) an income stream — either a fixed annual amount (CRAT) or a percentage of the trust's annual value (CRUT) — for a set period (up to 20 years) or for life.

**3. Termination.** At the end of the income phase, whatever's left in the trust passes to a designated charity (or charities).

## Why it's tax-efficient

Three things happen at funding that make a CRT economically powerful:

**1. The trust sells the asset tax-free.** Because the trust is a charitable entity, it can sell the appreciated asset and reinvest the full proceeds without immediate capital gains tax. On a $1M appreciated stock position with $200K basis, that's roughly $190K of immediate avoided federal capital gains tax (23.8% × $800K).

**2. You get an immediate charitable deduction.** The deduction equals the present value of the eventual remainder gift to charity — calculated using IRS-provided actuarial tables based on your age, the income payout rate, and prevailing interest rates. Typically 30–60% of the funding value.

**3. The income stream is tax-favored.** Distributions to the income beneficiary are taxed under specific ordering rules — ordinary income first, then capital gains, then tax-exempt income, then return of principal. For most CRTs holding appreciated assets, this means meaningful annual income with a lower effective tax rate than ordinary income.

## A concrete example

A 65-year-old client with a $2M concentrated stock position with $400K cost basis is considering selling. Direct sale:

- Capital gains tax: ~$381K (23.8% × $1.6M)
- Net proceeds invested: $1.62M
- At 5% withdrawal: $81K/year of post-tax income

CRT alternative — funding the entire $2M:

- Capital gains tax at funding: $0
- Charitable deduction: ~$700K–$900K (depending on payout rate, age, and current interest rates)
- Tax savings on deduction at 37% bracket: ~$260K–$330K
- Annual income from CRT: 5% of trust value = $100K (if CRUT) or fixed annual amount (if CRAT)
- Eventual remainder to charity: whatever's left at termination — typically $1M+ at reasonable assumptions

Net comparison: the CRT generates significantly higher annual income (because the full $2M is invested vs. $1.62M after tax), provides a large up-front deduction, and ultimately benefits a chosen charity instead of the IRS.

The tradeoff: you give up the ability to leave the principal to heirs. The asset goes to charity at the end.

## When a CRT makes sense

The structure works well when several conditions align:

- **Highly appreciated asset.** The bigger the embedded gain, the more meaningful the tax savings at funding.
- **Charitable intent already exists.** A CRT only works if you genuinely want a portion of the wealth to go to charity. Otherwise the structure is overkill.
- **Lifetime income is valuable.** You want or need an income stream — CRTs work well as a retirement income vehicle.
- **The asset can be sold.** Some assets (very illiquid private holdings, collectibles with no market) don't fit cleanly.
- **You have other assets for heirs.** The CRT ultimately benefits charity. If heirs would otherwise inherit the asset, you need other estate components for them.

## When a CRT doesn't make sense

- **Modest appreciated assets.** Setup and ongoing costs ($5K–$15K to establish, plus annual administration) don't justify the structure for funding amounts below $300K–$500K.
- **No charitable intent.** Don't use a CRT just for the tax benefit. The structure is irrevocable; the eventual remainder goes to charity.
- **Need for liquidity.** Once funded, you can't access the principal — only the income stream.
- **Plan to leave the asset to heirs.** The [step-up in basis at death](/articles/step-up-in-basis) wipes out the embedded gain anyway. CRTs are appropriate when sale during life is the plan.
- **Want simpler alternatives.** A [donor-advised fund](/articles/donor-advised-funds) with appreciated stock contribution is simpler and may achieve a meaningful portion of the goal without the CRT complexity.

## The variations

**CRAT (Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust):** Pays a fixed dollar amount annually. Simpler. Doesn't grow over time, so the income stream loses purchasing power to inflation.

**CRUT (Charitable Remainder Unitrust):** Pays a percentage of trust value annually, recalculated each year. More flexible. Income grows with the trust value, providing inflation protection but variable income.

**NIM-CRUT (Net Income with Makeup):** Pays the lesser of trust income or the unitrust amount, with makeup provisions. Useful for assets that produce variable income.

**Flip CRUT:** Operates as a NIM-CRUT initially (useful if the contributed asset is illiquid or non-income-producing) and "flips" to a standard CRUT once a triggering event occurs (often the sale of the original asset). Useful for funding with hard-to-value assets like real estate.

For most HNW retirees, the standard CRUT is the right choice.

## The administrative reality

CRTs require:

- An attorney experienced with CRTs to draft the trust document
- A trustee — can be a corporate trustee (typical for HNW families) or a trusted individual
- Annual valuations and tax filings (Form 5227)
- Investment management of the trust portfolio
- Coordination with the eventual charitable beneficiary

Costs run $3K–$10K annually for ongoing administration, plus investment management fees. For larger CRTs, this is modest relative to the underlying value. For smaller CRTs, the administration drag becomes meaningful.

## CRT vs. simpler alternatives

For households with charitable intent but uncertain whether a CRT is the right structure, consider in order:

1. **Direct gifts of appreciated stock to a [DAF](/articles/donor-advised-funds).** Simple, immediate deduction, no income stream but no administrative complexity.
2. **[QCDs from IRAs](/articles/qcds-explained) after age 70½.** Excludes income from AGI, simpler than a CRT for retirees focused on annual giving.
3. **Bunched DAF contributions.** Combines the [bunching strategy](/articles/bunching-charitable-deductions) with deferred granting.
4. **CRT.** When the income stream and the up-front deduction both matter, and the asset is large enough to justify the complexity.
5. **Private foundation.** When the family wants long-term governance and the wealth level supports the setup costs ($10M+ typically).

## The conversation worth having

If you have a highly appreciated asset, a desire for lifetime income, and a charitable intent that includes leaving substantial wealth to charity, a CRT may be worth modeling. The math is highly individual — payout rates, age, asset basis, and current interest rates all matter.
